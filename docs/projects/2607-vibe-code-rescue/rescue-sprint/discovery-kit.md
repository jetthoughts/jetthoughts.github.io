# Discovery Kit - Free Code Audit call

This is the script and the scorecard Paul runs on the free 45-minute Code Audit - the step between a booked call and a rescue proposal. The founder on the other end got burned once already, so the whole call is built to make them feel understood and leave with a straight answer, whether or not they ever hire us.

Two things ground every question below:
- The [Four Forces](customer-profile-four-forces.md) - Push and Pull pull the founder toward change, Anxiety and Habit hold them back. Paul amplifies the first pair and listens for the second, and never argues.
- The [D3 intake](assumptions-register.md) - five things Paul has to know before he can promise an audit he can actually deliver. They're folded into the conversation, not fired as a checklist.

---

## 45-minute agenda

| Time | Segment | What Paul is doing |
|---|---|---|
| 0-5 | Warm open | Lower the guard. Name the frame: this call is theirs, the write-up is theirs to keep. No pitch coming. |
| 5-13 | Situation | Get the founder walking him through what they have and what happened. Listen for the founder's own words about the break (Push). |
| 13-23 | Pain and impact | Find what the break actually cost - the customer, the raise, the sleep. This is where the founder feels understood. |
| 23-33 | The five things Paul needs | Who built it, what's broken now, whether they can get to the code, whether they own the servers, what clock they're on. The ownership questions carry the wedge (Pull). |
| 33-40 | Salvage or rebuild | Set up what the written verdict will and won't say. Break the "one more sprint" reflex (Habit). |
| 40-45 | What happens next | The write-up lands in a couple of days either way. If they want the fix, name the fixed price and the read-access they'll send. |

Paul writes the one-page assessment in the 10-minute buffer after the call, using the template at the bottom of this doc.

---

## The call, segment by segment

Each block below gives Paul what he's trying to surface, a suggested way in, and a few real questions he can pick from. He should ask two or three per segment, not all of them - it's a conversation, not a form. The force tag in italics is for Paul's ear, not to be read aloud.

### 0-5 - Warm open

Get the founder to relax before anything technical. A burned founder walks in expecting to be sold to, so the first ninety seconds have to prove that isn't happening.

Suggested way in: *"Before we open anything up, I want to say the obvious - this call is yours. One of our senior engineers reads your actual code, you get a plain-English write-up, and it's yours whether or not we ever work together. So let's just talk. What made you book this week?"*

- "What made this the week you looked for help, versus a month ago?" *(Push - the struggling moment)*
- "How did you find us - did someone point you here, or were you searching for a way to fix this?" *(reads the trigger + warmth)*

### 5-13 - Situation

Get the picture of what they have, in their words, before Paul forms any opinion. SPIN situation questions - low-threat, factual, and they let the founder narrate the break themselves.

Suggested way in: *"Walk me through it like I've never seen the app. When a brand-new person signs up today and starts clicking around, what happens?"*

- "When did it stop feeling like it was heading in the right direction?" *(Push)*
- "What's the thing you keep checking to make sure it hasn't broken again?" *(Push - surfaces the fear through behavior, not a feelings question)*
- "Your developers - when you ask what's going on, what do they tell you?" *(Push - listens for the "refactoring / architecture" stall)*

### 13-23 - Pain and impact

This is the segment that earns the founder's trust. Paul stops gathering facts and finds what the break actually cost. SPIN implication and need-payoff questions - the founder connects the technical mess to the money, the customers, and the reputation on the line.

Suggested way in: *"Okay. So it breaks. Help me understand what that's actually costing you - not the code, the real fallout."*

- "When it broke in front of a real user, what happened next - did you lose them, or was it worse than that?" *(Push - functional cost)*
- "Who else is watching this land? Investors, a cofounder, your first paying customers - and what have you had to tell them so far?" *(Anxiety - the social job, looking like you got taken)*
- "If nothing changes, where does this go in three months?" *(Push - raises the urgency without Paul having to)*
- "Be straight with me - do you actually trust what your team is telling you about how close this is?" *(Anxiety - the "am I being lied to" fear, surfaced, not argued)*

### 23-33 - The five things Paul needs

Everything in the [D3 intake](assumptions-register.md) has to come out here, but the founder should feel like Paul is uncovering problems on their behalf, not qualifying them as a lead. The ownership questions in particular land as "here's something you paid for and don't control" - that's the wedge no competitor leads with.

Suggested way in: *"I've got a picture of the pain. Now I need five practical things so the audit I send you is worth reading. Some of these you'll know cold, some you might have to check - both answers are useful."*

1. "Who actually built this - a dev shop, a freelancer, or one of the AI tools like Lovable, Cursor, or Bolt that write the code for you?" *(Push - build origin; tells Paul what kind of mess to expect)*
2. "Of everything that feels shaky, what's the one piece that would hurt the most if it broke tomorrow morning?" *(Push - ranks the damage)*
3. "Where does the code actually live? GitHub is the usual place - it's the online folder your app's code sits in. Can you log into that account yourself right now, or does your developer hold the keys?" *(Pull - the ownership wedge; also the gate: the audit needs read access to run)*
4. "Same question for the servers. The thing your app runs on is usually an Amazon account, AWS for short. Is that account in your name, or your developer's?" *(Pull - ownership; surfaces the second hostage risk)*
5. "What's the clock on this? A launch date, a raise you're closing, a customer who's threatening to walk - anything pushing you?" *(Push - urgency and timeline pressure)*

If the founder can't get into the GitHub or the servers, that's not a dead end - Paul names it as the first thing the audit will help them fix, and the call stays useful.

### 33-40 - Salvage or rebuild

The founder is braced for the worst answer - "throw it all out and start over" - and that fear is exactly what keeps them stuck with the shop they don't trust. Paul defuses it by setting realistic expectations for the written verdict before he's even written it.

Suggested way in: *"Here's what you're going to get from me in a couple of days. A one-pager, no jargon, that tells you what's solid, what's fragile, and one honest call - keep what you have and fix the broken parts, or replace the parts that can't hold the product you're describing."*

- "Most founders I talk to assume the answer is 'scrap everything.' On the last few rescues we kept somewhere between a third and half of the original code. Would knowing exactly which half change how this feels?" *(Habit - reframes the sunk-cost trap without a slogan)*
- "The 'one more sprint will fix it' bet - how many times have you already made it?" *(Habit - names the loop the founder is stuck in)*
- "And if the honest answer turned out to be 'this is salvageable, you don't need a full rebuild' - would you believe me, given how the last team talked to you?" *(Anxiety - lets the founder voice the distrust so the free write-up can answer it)*

### 40-45 - What happens next

Close on clarity, not commitment. The founder should hang up knowing exactly what they'll receive and when, with zero pressure to decide.

Suggested way in: *"That's everything I need. Here's what happens now, and there's no catch in it."*

- The write-up lands in a couple of business days - what's solid, what to fix first, salvage or rebuild. Theirs to keep.
- The one action Paul asks for: read-only access to the repo, so a senior engineer reads the real code, not a guess. If they can't get it yet, Paul sends the steps to claw it back.
- If they want the fix after reading it, Paul walks them through the rescue - fixed price agreed up front, 4 to 8 weeks, ownership handed back at each milestone. No number that creeps.

Suggested close: *"Even if you read the write-up and decide to stay where you are, you'll finally know where you actually stand. That's the point of it."*

## One-page Rescue Audit - deliverable template

Paul fills this in during the 10-minute buffer and sends it as the promised write-up. Plain English, one page, no jargon the founder has to look up. Each row gets a Red, Amber, or Green and one sentence a non-technical founder can act on.

**Prepared for:** [Founder name] / [Company] - [date]
**Reviewed by:** [Senior engineer] with Paul Keen, fractional CTO

### Scorecard

| What we checked | In plain English | Rating | The one line that matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ownership | Do you hold the code and the servers, or does someone else? | 🔴/🟡/🟢 | [e.g. "The code is in your developer's GitHub, not yours - you can't move it without them today."] |
| Security | Can one user reach another user's data? Are passwords and keys exposed? | 🔴/🟡/🟢 | [e.g. "Any logged-in user can open another customer's account by changing the number in the web address."] |
| Does anything catch breakage before your users do? | Are there automated tests, or does every bug reach a real person first? | 🔴/🟡/🟢 | [e.g. "Almost nothing is tested, so a change in one place quietly breaks another - which is what you've been living."] |
| Salvageable | How much of what you paid for is worth keeping? | ~[N]% | [e.g. "Roughly 40% is solid. The login, payments, and data layer need replacing."] |
| What breaks next | If nothing changes, what's the most likely next failure? | 🔴/🟡/🟢 | [e.g. "The database has no backups - one bad deploy could wipe your customer data with no way back."] |

### The honest call

> **Verdict: [ Salvage / Fix the broken parts / Rebuild from scratch ]**
>
> [Two or three plain sentences. Name the choice and why. Example: "Keep it. About half of what you have is worth building on, and the broken half - login, payments, and the way data is stored - can be rebuilt on a solid foundation in 4 to 8 weeks. Starting over would cost you more and buy you nothing you don't already have."]
>
> [If rebuild: say so plainly, even though salvage is the easier sale for us. Example: "Rebuild the core. The foundation can't carry the product you're describing, and every fix on top of it will cost more than it saves. You'd keep your design and your data - not the code underneath."]

### Recommended next step

[One clear action. Usually one of:]
- "Get read access to your GitHub and AWS accounts first - here are the exact steps. You should hold these no matter what you decide."
- "If you want us to handle the fix, the next step is a 30-minute scoping call and a fixed-price proposal - no surprise number."
- "If you'd rather your current team fix it, take this scorecard to them and ask for a plan against these five points. You'll know fast whether they can."

## Cold-eyes (self-refute)

**The strongest case that this script backfires:** A founder who just got burned is allergic to being handled. Three moves in this draft risk tripping that wire. First, the five-question intake in the 23-33 block can feel like a vendor qualifying a lead - "can you get access to the code" reads as Paul checking whether the deal is workable for *him*, not helping *them*. Second, the pain segment leans on emotional questions ("who's watching this land," "do you trust what your team tells you"); a defensive founder can hear rapport-hacking, the exact warmth a bad agency used on them last time. Third, the call ends by naming a fixed price and read-access - a pull toward the sale that can retroactively make the whole warm open feel like a setup.

**Does it survive?** Mostly, with the changes below. The intake questions survive because they're framed as the founder's problem, not Paul's qualification - the ownership questions in particular hand the founder a problem they didn't know they had ("you paid for this and don't control it"), which is the opposite of a vendor probe. The pain questions survive because they're tied to observable consequences (a lost customer, an investor update) rather than feelings, and because the write-up is genuinely theirs to keep - the 90/10 promise is real, not a frame. The close survives because the price only comes up if the founder wants the fix, and the default next step Paul offers is "go get your own access back," which is useful even if they never hire us.

**What I changed off the back of this:**
- Moved the ownership questions to *after* the pain is established, so they read as "here's what's wrong" rather than "are you a qualified buyer."
- Cut the one straight feelings question ("how are you sleeping") and replaced it with a behavior question - "what do you keep checking to make sure it hasn't broken" - which surfaces the same fear without therapist voice.
- Made the intake explicitly a conversation, not a numbered volley Paul reads down, and gave him an off-ramp when the founder can't get repo access so the call never dead-ends.
- Anchored the salvage reframe in a real number ("a third to half of the original code") instead of a reassuring slogan.

**Verdict: PASS.** The script makes the founder feel understood and sends them away with a straight answer they can use alone. The one live risk to watch on real calls: if Paul rushes the pain segment to get to the five questions, the intake will feel like qualifying. The agenda gives pain ten minutes before the intake starts on purpose - hold that order.
