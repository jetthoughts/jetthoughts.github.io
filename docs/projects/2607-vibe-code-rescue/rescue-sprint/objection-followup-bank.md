# Objection + Follow-up Bank

> T9 output - grounded in discovery-kit.md, customer-profile-four-forces.md, and voice-of-customer.md. For Paul's Code Audit calls and outbound follow-ups. Use `-` not `--`, no banned AI patterns.

---

## How to use this file

Each objection below has: the founder's likely words, the force driving it (Anxiety or Habit), what NOT to say, and what TO say. Paul picks the response that fits the moment - never fires them all.

The 3-touch no-reply sequence lives at the bottom. It works for founders who booked a call but went quiet, or who replied to an opener but didn't book.

---

## Objection bank

### 1. "Why not just re-hire the shop / give them one more chance?"

**Force**: Habit (sunk cost + known quantity)

**What NOT to say**: "They already failed you." (Attacking their past decision makes them defensive.)

**What TO say**: "That's a fair question. Before you commit another dollar to them, let one of our senior engineers read the code for free and tell you what you actually have. If it turns out they did good work and just need a runway extension, the write-up will say that. You keep it either way."

**Why it works**: The free audit removes the risk of looking. Paul isn't asking them to fire the current shop - he's offering a second opinion that costs nothing. The founder can use the write-up to pressure the current shop to perform, which is useful even if they never hire JetThoughts.

---

### 2. "Is $7,500 real, or does it balloon like last time?"

**Force**: Anxiety (burned by scope creep before)

**What NOT to say**: "We're different." (Every shop says that.)

**What TO say**: "The price is fixed and agreed in writing before we start. If the scope changes - and sometimes it does once we open the code up - we stop, show you what we found, and you decide whether to adjust. The number doesn't creep without you knowing and agreeing. And if you just want to test the waters first, the triage sprint is $2,500 - you see real results before committing more."

**Why it works**: It names the exact fear (creeping costs) and answers with a mechanism (written agreement + stop-and-ask). Not a promise, a process.

---

### 3. "Will you burn me too?"

**Force**: Anxiety (trust broken once, now allergic to promises)

**What NOT to say**: "We have 4.8/5 from 32 clients." (Proof helps later, but leading with it sounds like every other pitch.)

**What TO say**: "I'd be surprised if you weren't skeptical. That's why the first step is free and the write-up is yours to keep - you judge us by what we actually find in your code, not by what we say about ourselves on a sales call. If the write-up doesn't feel honest, you walk away with a better understanding of your own app."

**Why it works**: Names the emotion (skeptical) without being condescending. Points to the mechanism (free audit, their write-up) rather than asking for blind trust. The founder judges by evidence, not promises.

---

### 4. "One more sprint and it'll be fine."

**Force**: Habit (sunk cost + hope loop)

**What NOT to say**: "That's what you said last time." (True, but shaming.)

**What TO say**: "How many sprints has it been so far? If you're past two or three rounds of 'just one more fix,' the audit will give you a straight answer on whether the foundation can hold - or whether you're patching sand. You'll know in 45 minutes instead of another month."

**Why it works**: Asks the founder to count their own sprints (self-discovery, not Paul telling them). Offers the audit as a faster way to get the answer they're guessing at.

---

### 5. "I don't want to start over / lose everything I paid for."

**Force**: Habit (sunk cost fallacy)

**What NOT to say**: "Sometimes you have to cut your losses." (Sounds like a teardown pitch.)

**What TO say**: "You probably won't have to. On the last few rescues, we kept somewhere between a third and half of the original code. The parts that work, we keep. The parts that don't, we rebuild on a solid foundation. You're not throwing it away - you're keeping what's worth keeping and fixing what isn't."

**Why it works**: Gives a real number (30-50% kept) that reframes the choice from "all or nothing" to "keep the good, fix the bad." The founder doesn't feel like they wasted their money.

---

### 6. "Can you even work on my code if the shop holds the keys?"

**Force**: Anxiety (ownership hostage - Trigger 3)

**What NOT to say**: "We'll figure it out." (Vague.)

**What TO say**: "That's actually the first thing the audit checks. If you don't hold the code and the servers, we'll show you the exact steps to get access back - and that's useful whether or not you hire us. You should own what you paid for."

**Why it works**: Turns the objection into a reason to do the audit. The ownership wedge is our strongest differentiator - no competitor leads with "you should own your code." The founder gets value (access steps) even if they never become a client.

---

### 7. "What if it's not salvageable and I waste more money?"

**Force**: Anxiety (double-loss fear)

**What NOT to say**: "We'll make it work." (Overpromising is what burned them.)

**What TO say**: "If the honest answer is 'rebuild the core,' we'll tell you that - even though a salvage job would be easier for us to sell. The write-up will say exactly what's worth keeping and what isn't, and you can take that to any team, not just us."

**Why it works**: Names the possibility of a rebuild honestly. The "even though salvage is easier to sell" line proves Paul isn't optimizing for the sale. The write-up is transferable, which lowers the stakes.

---

### 8. "I don't have time to deal with this right now."

**Force**: Habit (firefighting leaves no room to evaluate)

**What NOT to say**: "This only takes 45 minutes." (Minimizes their real constraint.)

**What TO say**: "The audit is one call and the write-up shows up a couple days later. You don't have to do anything between now and then. Most founders who book it are firefighting too - the 45 minutes is the one hour where someone else looks at the problem for you."

**Why it works**: Acknowledges the constraint (firefighting) and reframes the audit as relief from it, not another task on the pile.

---

### 9. "I already got a quote for $55K to rebuild. Why is yours different?"

**Force**: Anxiety (comparison shopping, can't evaluate quotes)

**What NOT to say**: "We're cheaper." (Race to the bottom.)

**What TO say**: "Before you sign anything, let one of our engineers read the code for free and tell you whether that $55K quote matches what's actually in there. Our rescue is $7,500 because we use AI-assisted development to move faster - same senior oversight, fraction of the cost. And if you just want proof first, the triage sprint is $2,500 and you'll see real fixes in 48 hours."

**Why it works**: Doesn't dismiss the competitor quote. Positions the free audit as due diligence before any commitment, which is valuable regardless of who does the work.

## 3-touch no-reply follow-up sequence

For founders who booked a call but went quiet, or who replied to an opener but didn't book. Space each touch 3-4 business days apart. Stop after three.

### Touch 1 (day 0, right after the silence)

**Subject / opener**: "Still happy to look at the code"

> No pressure. The Code Audit slot is still yours if you want it - one of our senior engineers reads your actual codebase and you get a one-page write-up in plain English. It's yours whether or not we ever work together.
>
> If the timing isn't right, just say the word and I'll check back in a few weeks instead.

**Why**: Reminds them the audit is free and theirs to keep. Offers an easy out ("check back in a few weeks") so they don't feel cornered.

### Touch 2 (day 7-10)

**Subject / opener**: "Quick question"

> One thing I've been meaning to ask - do you actually hold the login to your GitHub and your server account (AWS or wherever it runs), or does your developer keep those for you?
>
> If you're not sure, that's actually one of the first things we check on the audit. A lot of founders don't realize they don't own what they paid for until they try to move.

**Why**: Uses a specific, useful question (ownership) as the hook. Not "just checking in" - gives them a reason to reply even if they don't book. The ownership wedge is the strongest differentiator.

### Touch 3 (day 14-18, final touch)

**Subject / opener**: "Last note from me"

> I won't keep pinging you. Just wanted to leave you with this - if your app breaks in front of a real user or an investor asks a question you can't answer, you know where to find us. The audit is always free, and the write-up is always yours.
>
> No hard feelings either way. Hope the product gets where it's going.

**Why**: Clean close. Names the two struggling moments (user break, investor question) without being dramatic. Leaves the door open without pressure. "No hard feelings" signals respect, which is what a burned founder needs to hear.

## LIGHT self-refute

**The strongest case this bank backfires**: A founder who got burned reads "we're different" energy in every response, no matter how it's framed. The free audit itself can feel like a setup - "free" is what the last shop called their discovery call before the $25K landed.

**Does it survive?** Mostly. Each objection response points to a mechanism (written agreement, stop-and-ask, transferable write-up) rather than a promise. The founder evaluates the mechanism, not the claim. The follow-up sequence is designed to be useful even if the founder never books - the ownership question in Touch 2 is a real thing they should check.

**What to watch on real calls**: If Paul fires multiple objection responses in sequence, it sounds like a script. Pick the one that matches what the founder actually said, and let the silence do the work after.

**Verdict**: PASS. Each response dismantles one specific fear with one specific mechanism. The follow-up sequence escalates from "still available" to "here's something useful" to "clean close" without ever getting pushy.
