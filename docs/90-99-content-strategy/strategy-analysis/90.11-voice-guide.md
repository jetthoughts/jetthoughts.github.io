# JetThoughts Voice Guide — ICP-E Content

**Status:** Canonical voice reference for all founder-facing content
**Last Updated:** 2026-04-11
**Applies to:** Blog posts, landing pages, emails, social posts targeting ICP-E (non-technical founders)
**Voice formula:** Rob Walling's directness (70%) + Rand Fishkin's vulnerability (30%)

---

## The Voice in One Sentence

Write like a founder who's rescued 50 projects talking to another founder at a bar — not a consulting firm publishing a whitepaper.

---

## 1. Tone Spectrum

| We ARE | We are NOT |
|---|---|
| Direct, blunt, specific | Vague, hedging, diplomatic |
| Empathetic (we've seen this before) | Sympathetic (poor you) |
| Peer-to-peer (founder talking to founder) | Vendor-to-prospect (agency pitching client) |
| Self-aware (we make mistakes too) | Infallible (we always get it right) |
| Conversational (contractions, fragments) | Formal (one should consider) |
| Opinionated (this is what works) | Neutral (there are many approaches) |

### What Rob Walling sounds like
> "I hired the wrong person. Twice. Here's what I finally got right."

He's practical. No drama. States the problem, states what he did, states the result. Midwest directness — respects the reader's time. Uses exact numbers ("$4,500/month", "6 weeks", "3 developers"). Never says "it depends" without saying what it depends ON.

### What Rand Fishkin sounds like
> "We spent $40K on an agency redesign. The result was worse than what we started with. I should have listened to my gut."

He's emotionally honest without being dramatic. Admits mistakes publicly. Uses specific dollar amounts. Writes like he's confiding a hard lesson, not publishing a case study. Self-deprecating without undermining authority.

### The JetThoughts hybrid
> "We took over a project last month. The founder had spent $80K and six months. Test coverage: 2%. No error handling. The first real user crashed the app. We've seen this exact pattern forty times. Here's what we do in the first 48 hours."

Direct (Walling) + vulnerable ("we've seen this") + specific ($80K, 2%, 48 hours) + actionable (what we do).

## 2. Sentence-Level Rules

### DO

**Use sentence fragments for emphasis.**
> The code looked clean. Tests: zero. Documentation: none. Error handling: prayer-based.

**Use contractions. Always.**
> "You're not crazy" not "You are not irrational"
> "They won't tell you" not "They will not disclose"
> "Here's what works" not "The following approach is recommended"

**Lead with the punch, explain after.**
> "Fire them. Here's how to do it safely." (not "In this guide, we'll explore the process of transitioning away from your current development partner.")

**Use exact numbers instead of vague ranges.**
> "$12K/month" not "affordable rates"
> "2% test coverage" not "low test coverage"
> "48 hours" not "quickly"
> "14 critical vulnerabilities" not "security issues"

**Use "you" and "your" — talk TO the reader.**
> "Your agency isn't ghosting you. It's hiding bad code."

**Use rhetorical questions that sting.**
> "If they push back on repo access — ask yourself why. What are they protecting?"

**Interrupt yourself with asides.**
> "The MVP was delivered on time — the founder was thrilled. The app loaded. The demo looked great. Then real users showed up."

**Admit what we don't know or can't do.**
> "Sometimes the dev shop isn't the problem. Sometimes it's the scope. Be honest with yourself."

### DON'T

**Never hedge when you have an opinion.**
> ❌ "It may be advisable to consider terminating the engagement"
> ✅ "Fire them."

**Never use passive voice to avoid accountability.**
> ❌ "Mistakes were made in the initial hiring process"
> ✅ "You hired wrong. So did we, the first three times."

**Never open with context when you can open with conflict.**
> ❌ "In today's competitive startup landscape, choosing the right development partner is crucial."
> ✅ "Your agency isn't ghosting you. It's hiding bad code."

**Never list things in perfectly parallel structure.**
AI writes clean parallel lists. Humans don't. Mix formats:
> ❌ "Ask about X. The answer should be Y. Ask about A. The answer should be B."
> ✅ "Ask to see a weekly report — a real one, not a template. Then ask who owns the code. If they can't name a specific contract clause, they don't have one."

**Never use performative empathy.**
> ❌ "We understand how frustrating this must be for you."
> ✅ "I know how this feels. You hired a team, wrote them a check, waited three months — and now the demo sort of works."

## 3. Banned Words & Phrases

These words trigger AI detection and founder skepticism. Zero tolerance.

### Banned adjectives/adverbs
unlock, harness, leverage, seamless, robust, supercharge, revolutionize, cutting-edge, world-class, best-in-class, state-of-the-art, innovative, synergy, holistic, comprehensive (when used as filler)

### Banned openings
"In today's...", "When it comes to...", "It's worth noting...", "It's important to understand...", "As we all know...", "In the fast-paced world of..."

### Banned transitions
"That being said...", "With that in mind...", "Let's dive in...", "Without further ado...", "Moving on to...", "Now let's explore..."

### Banned closings
"In conclusion...", "To sum up...", "At the end of the day...", "The bottom line is..."

### Banned verbs
embark, delve, discover (when used as "discover how"), navigate (when used metaphorically), empower, transform (when used as marketing verb)

### Banned phrases
"game-changer", "deep dive", "the journey", "best practices" (unless quoting someone), "thought leader", "disruptive", "paradigm shift", "move the needle", "circle back", "low-hanging fruit"

### What to use instead

| Banned | Replacement |
|---|---|
| leverage | use |
| utilize | use |
| implement | set up / add / build |
| comprehensive guide | guide |
| robust solution | it works |
| seamless integration | it plugs in |
| cutting-edge | new / recent |
| empower your team | your team can now |
| drive results | here's what changed |
| unlock potential | do more with |

### Banned structural patterns (AI tells)

These patterns are invisible at the word level but scream "AI wrote this" at the paragraph level. Zero tolerance.

| Pattern | Example | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| Rule of three | "tests, reviews, and humans who take responsibility" | Combine two items, vary the third: "tests and reviews — plus someone who signs off" |
| Triple rhetorical questions | "Why? How? And what prevents it?" | One question max, or rephrase as a statement |
| Signposting | "This post is about..." / "Here's what you need to know" | Delete. Just say the thing. |
| Bold inline-header lists | "**Speed:** fast. **Quality:** good." | Write prose. Mix sentence lengths. |
| Negative parallelism | "It's not X — it's Y" / "Not just A, but B" | Pick one framing and commit: "It's Y." |
| Slogany parallel closings | "X is a sketch. Y is engineering." | Use a concrete image instead: "Use it for demos. Don't ship it to real users." |
| Therapist voice | "That's the hardest part" / "The next step is smaller than you think" | Name the concrete next step instead |
| Copula avoidance | "serves as" / "stands as" / "marks a" | Use "is" |
| Four+ parallel short sentences | "Names are clear. Functions are small. Comments exist. Structure follows patterns." | Vary: combine two into one longer sentence, break the rhythm |
| Perfectly consistent hyphenation | "cross-functional", "data-driven", "high-quality" every time | Humans are inconsistent. Drop some hyphens. |
| Impersonal fragment stacking | "No tests. Open endpoints. Corrupted data." | Rewrite with a person discovering or experiencing it: "We opened the codebase and there were no tests at all." |
| Noun stacking without subject | "React + Rails, clean conventions, the demo sailed through review." | Give it a human subject: "His dev shop had used React and Rails, kept the conventions clean, and the demo looked good enough that his investors didn't blink." |
| Sustained staccato (fragments OR short sentences) | **Fragments:** "No tests. Open endpoints. Corrupted data." **Complete sentences:** "You hired a team. You wrote them a check. Weeks pass." Both are rhythmically monotonous. Threshold: 3+ consecutive sentences under ~10 words with similar structure, regardless of grammar. | Combine into flowing clauses with commas, conjunctions, or varied lengths. E.g.: "You hired a team, wrote them a check, and weeks passed with nothing to show for it." One short punch is emphasis; three in a row is a pattern. |
| Command structure repetition | "Ask for the test coverage. Open the commit log. Pick any form. Ask your team." | Don't start 3+ consecutive paragraphs with imperatives. Vary: "Start with...", "Then look at...", "Try breaking..." → or better, embed in narrative: "The easiest check is test coverage. If yours is in single digits..." |
| Apologetic caveats | "While every project is different..." / "It depends on the use case..." | State observations as facts. The reader is smart enough to understand context. If you must qualify, be specific: "This applies to apps with 500+ daily users." |
| Telling instead of showing | "The error handling was bad" / "The code had security problems" | Describe the specific technical mechanic: "Anyone could access another user's data by changing the ID in the URL." Show the failure, don't label it. |
| Fluffy AI narration | "The alerts fire correctly for a full quarter. Then someone upgrades Rails..." | Replace generalized dramatic present-tense with specific past-tense incident: name the client (anonymized), version, timeline, exact failure. |
| Fake authority generalization | "We've seen this on every codebase we've inherited" | Replace with specific count and detail: "The last three codebases we inherited all had this." |

### Punctuation

- Use regular dashes (`-`) not em dashes (`—`) for parenthetical asides and list intros
- Use commas or periods when a dash isn't needed

**The "who" test**: Read every sentence and ask "who is doing what?" If the answer is a thing, not a person, rewrite it with a human subject. Technical failures should be discovered by someone. Features should be built by someone. Symptoms should be experienced by someone.

**The "show" test**: Read every claim and ask "did I show the mechanic or just label it?" Replace adjectives ("bad", "broken", "insecure") with concrete scenarios a founder can picture.

**The coffee test**: Read the paragraph aloud. If it sounds like a keynote speech or a Wikipedia article, rewrite it. If it sounds like someone explaining this over coffee, keep it.

### Education-to-promotion ratio

90% of the post must be purely educational. The reader should learn something useful even if they never contact JetThoughts. Promotion (CTA, service mentions) stays under 10%, ideally in one paragraph near the end. If a reader feels "sold to" before the final section, the ratio is wrong.

### Trade-offs and honesty

Always acknowledge what a proposed solution fails to do or what it costs. AI presents solutions as universally perfect. Humans know every decision is a compromise.

> Bad: "TDD prevents all these problems."
> Good: "TDD catches most of these failures before production. It also slows down the first sprint by 20-30%, which is why shops under deadline pressure skip it."

### Narrative digression

Break predictable structure by interrupting a technical explanation with a brief human observation, then return to the analysis. This mimics how people actually think and talk.

> "The AI had built a login screen and left every other door wide open. (We see this pattern so often we have a name for it internally: 'front door only' architecture.) A basic penetration test found that any logged-in user could read, edit, or delete another user's account."

## 4. Structural Patterns

### Opening (first 3 paragraphs)

**Paragraph 1: The hook.** One or two sentences. State a painful truth or a contrarian claim. No context, no background, no "let me tell you about..."

**Paragraph 2: The recognition.** Show you understand the reader's specific situation. Use "you" language. Include a specific detail that makes them think "this person has been in my office."

**Paragraph 3: The promise.** What this post will give them. Be specific — not "a guide" but "the exact steps, including what to do tonight."

### Body sections

**Every H2 should be scannable on mobile.** A stressed founder will scroll through headings first. Each H2 must communicate value without reading the section.

> ❌ "Key Considerations"
> ✅ "5 Signs It's Time to Fire Your Agency"

**Mix formats within sections.** Don't make every section a bulleted list OR all prose. Alternate: prose paragraph → bold call-out → short list → prose with specific example.

**Include one "But wait" objection per post.** Anticipate what the reader is thinking and address it directly:
> "But I signed a contract!" you say. Yes. And it probably doesn't protect you.

### CTAs

**Position at the moment of maximum motivation, not the end.** After the reader recognizes their problem (post-diagnosis), not after they've read everything (post-fatigue).

**Be specific about what they get.**
> ❌ "Contact us to learn more"
> ✅ "We do a free 45-minute code audit: one senior developer, your codebase, a written one-page assessment. No contract, no follow-up calls."

**Name the deliverable.**
> "a written one-page assessment" — the reader knows exactly what they'll receive.

### Closing

**No summaries.** The reader just read the post. Don't restate the key points.

**Reframe, don't recap.** Turn the problem into a decision:
> "Firing a dev shop is not a failure. Staying with a bad one is."

## 5. Proof Points & Specificity

### Always use specific numbers over vague claims

| Vague (don't) | Specific (do) |
|---|---|
| "We have extensive experience" | "We've rescued 40+ codebases in 20 years" |
| "Quick turnaround" | "48-hour developer start time" |
| "Affordable pricing" | "$5K-$30K/month, milestone billing" |
| "High test coverage" | "70%+ test coverage, TDD from day one" |
| "Transparent process" | "Weekly one-page report every Friday: what shipped, what's blocked, what's next" |

### Always cite sources for statistics

Every number in a post must link to its source. No exceptions. If you can't find a source, either find a different stat or remove the claim entirely.

> ✅ "Qodo's 2025 report found AI-generated code produces [1.7x more issues](https://www.qodo.ai/reports/state-of-ai-code-quality/)"
> ❌ "Studies show that AI-generated code has more bugs"

### Use real (anonymized) stories over hypotheticals

> ❌ "Imagine a founder who hired a dev shop and it didn't work out..."
> ✅ "A founder came to us after six months with a vibe coding shop. Test coverage: 2%. First real user crashed the app."

## 7. Voice by Content Stream

### Founders stream (ICP-E direct)
Full voice guide (sections 1-6) applies. Maximum empathy, zero jargon, specific dollar amounts, "I've been where you are" energy. Reference voice: Rob Walling + Rand Fishkin.

### Rails / Tech stream (CTO/senior dev audience) — Thoughtbot Style

Reference voice: [thoughtbot blog](https://thoughtbot.com/blog). Study these posts:
- [Turbo morphing woes](https://thoughtbot.com/blog/turbo-morphing-woes)
- [Sixty-six commits and the commands that didn't survive](https://thoughtbot.com/blog/sixty-six-commits-and-the-commands-that-didnt-survive)
- [Enhancing job reliability with Sidekiq Pro's super fetch](https://thoughtbot.com/blog/enhancing-job-reliability-with-sidekiq-pro-s-super-fetch-strategy)

**The 10 Rules of Thoughtbot Tech Voice:**

**1. Open with tension, not explanation.**
> ✅ "Your background jobs lie to you." (paradox, makes you read the next line)
> ❌ "This article explains Active Job Continuations in Rails 8.1." (feature announcement)

**2. Vary sentence length like rhythm — short punch, then explanation.**
> ✅ "Problem solved! Let's enable Turbo morphing and see what happens." Then longer: "We can't use `data-turbo-permanent` here because the element is dynamically generated."
> ❌ Every sentence is 15-20 words. Consistent. Monotonous. The reader's brain tunes out.

**3. Introduce code with a conversational preface, not a header.**
> ✅ "Here's what the configuration looks like:" or "The fix is one line:"
> ❌ "## Code Example" or "The following implementation demonstrates:"

**4. Use "we" and "I" — own the opinion.**
> ✅ "I found out what 'useful' actually meant" / "We shipped this to production last month"
> ❌ "It was discovered that..." / "One might consider..." / "It is recommended to..."

**5. Flag caveats upfront, not buried.**
> ✅ "Morphing is a sharp knife. Here are the five ways it'll cut you." (stated early, then proven)
> ❌ Footnote: "Note: this approach has some limitations in certain edge cases."

**6. Kill failed ideas without defensiveness.**
> ✅ "Both tried to codify processes that worked better as conversations. So they died."
> ❌ "These commands had some limitations and were eventually deprecated."

**7. Ground abstractions in real examples — clients, metrics, commits.**
> ✅ "Healthcare client using RubyLLM in production..." / "Sixty-six commits in seven days"
> ❌ "Consider a hypothetical application that needs to process background jobs..."

**8. Acknowledge tradeoffs with cost-benefit honesty.**
> ✅ "This increases resiliency, but at the cost of increased network traffic. When job loss threatens significant impact, the tradeoff pays off."
> ❌ "This is the best approach." (no caveats)

**9. Close with forward guidance, not pressure.**
> ✅ "Start with the commands you'd use today" + link to gem + related reading
> ❌ "Contact us to learn more!" or just stopping abruptly

**10. Show personality through specificity, not commentary.**
> ✅ Choosing a precise example that reveals taste / "That feels right"
> ❌ "In my humble opinion..." / "I personally believe..."

**What makes thoughtbot voice work:**
- Trusts the reader's intelligence (doesn't explain basic jargon to senior devs)
- Shares craft decisions transparently (what they tried, what failed, what they shipped)
- Leads with problems, not solutions
- Treats code as narrative evidence, not isolated reference
- Not formal enough to feel distant, never casual enough to feel sloppy

**Common failures in our tech posts (from review of last 5):**
- Openings announce features instead of naming problems: "Rails 8 introduces Solid Cache" → should be "Redis is the most expensive line item on your infrastructure bill. Rails 8 fixes that."
- Neutral Wikipedia tone instead of opinionated: "BCrypt has been the default for over a decade" → should be "BCrypt dominated for a decade, but Argon2id is objectively better against GPU attacks — the security community moved on."
- No internal links to related JetThoughts posts
- Missing explicit "When NOT to use this" sections

### AI stream (founders + CTOs)
Fishkin vulnerability (50%) + Walling directness (50%). Name the vibe coding crisis. Show specific damage. Link to JetThoughts practices as the antidote. "This is what went wrong. This is why. This is how to not repeat it."

## 8. Quick Self-Test

### For Founder posts (ICP-E)

- [ ] Does paragraph 1 state a painful truth or contrarian claim? (not context)
- [ ] Would a non-technical founder understand every sentence?
- [ ] Are there 4+ specific numbers? ($, %, hours, count)
- [ ] Is there at least one "we screwed this up too" moment?
- [ ] Is there at least one "But wait—" objection handled?
- [ ] Do the lists vary in format? (not all perfectly parallel)
- [ ] Does the CTA name a specific deliverable?
- [ ] Would you send this to a friend who got burned? (the ultimate test)
- [ ] Zero banned words?
- [ ] Does it sound like Rob Walling wrote it?

### For Tech posts (Rails/AI stream)

- [ ] Does paragraph 1 open with tension — a problem, paradox, or bold claim? (not "Rails 8 introduces...")
- [ ] Is there a before/after code comparison?
- [ ] Does the author have an opinion? ("This is better because X" not "there are multiple approaches")
- [ ] Are code blocks introduced conversationally? ("Here's the fix:" not "## Implementation")
- [ ] Is there a "When NOT to use this" section with honest tradeoffs?
- [ ] Are there real-world examples? (client stories, actual metrics, production data)
- [ ] 4+ internal links to related JetThoughts posts?
- [ ] Does the closing give forward guidance? (next steps, related reading)
- [ ] Zero feature-announcement openings? (no "X introduces Y")
- [ ] Does it sound like thoughtbot wrote it? (authoritative, opinionated, concise)
