Fourteen interviews where 12 people say "I would pay for that" feels like demand. Build the product, ship the landing page, and the same twelve people never type an email into the box. In this lesson you'll ship a real page cold strangers can click on, the cheapest demand signal you can buy, in one evening.

After this lesson you will be able to: **pick a no-code builder and ship a 6-element landing page from your hypothesis in under an hour.**

---

A smoke test answers one question: when a cold stranger sees your hypothesis as a headline, do they click the CTA? It does not answer whether the product works (you haven't built it), whether people will pay long-term, or whether your price holds. It catches ideas that read well on paper but produce zero clicks from a real audience.

**Pick your builder, one decision, not a research project:**

1. **Mixo** ([mixo.io](https://www.mixo.io/), $7/mo), purpose-built for this job: idea → site in ~30 seconds, email capture included. Start here.
2. **Manus AI** ([manus.im](https://manus.im/), free tier, 300 daily credits), highest output quality in 2026. Use if Mixo's templates don't fit.
3. **Durable** ([durable.com](https://durable.com/), free for 3 pages), **NeetoSite** ([neetosite.com](https://neetosite.com/), free tier), or **Carrd** ([carrd.co](https://carrd.co), free for 3 sites), only if you've used a builder before.

Pick one and ship. Don't comparison-shop for a week: you're testing demand, not builders.

> **The free-subdomain fear (ignore it).** A page on a free subdomain that solves a hot problem converts better than a beautiful custom-domain page that solves nothing. If your hypothesis is right, the subdomain won't stop clicks. If it's wrong, a $15 domain won't save it. Ship the ugly version first.

## Do this now

1. Open your builder of choice. Paste your Founding Hypothesis sentence as the prompt.
2. Fill in the 6 elements:
   - **Headline**: audience + outcome
   - **Sub-headline**: the mechanism
   - **Hero visual**: 15-second Loom, not a mockup
   - **Value props**: 3-4 benefit sentences (not feature names)
   - **One CTA**: "Get on the waitlist" + email field
   - **Footer disclaimer**: "Coming soon. Email signup reserves your spot at launch."

   Banned: pricing tables, FAQs, testimonials, blog pages, "How it works" sections. Every extra element waters down the CTA.
3. Cap design at 30 minutes. Record a 15-second Loom of you using an imagined version of your product. Swap it into the hero visual slot.
4. Open your page URL in an incognito window. **Success check:** Can a stranger read the headline and know within 3 seconds (a) who the product is for, (b) what it does? If yes, you're done. If no, tighten the headline.

**If this fails:** Your builder produces a page that doesn't make sense: generic copy, wrong audience, nothing resembling your hypothesis. **Why:** your hypothesis sentence is too vague for the AI to generate from. **Fix:** go back to your Founding Hypothesis and make the [customer] and [problem] blanks more specific. "Solo chiropractors" not "small business owners." "Insurance claim resubmission" not "billing issues." Repaste and regenerate.

---

Open your page in incognito. Look at it for 5 seconds, then close the tab. Write down the one thing you remember. If it's not the headline + CTA, those two elements need to be stronger.

> **Case Study: Mia**
>
> Mia opened NeetoSite and grabbed a screenshot from her desktop: her old WordPress booking system, a calendar widget showing "no availability" on every date, circled in red. She had spent $600 building it last year. It had booked zero tutors.
>
> She pasted her hypothesis into NeetoSite: "Parents spend 6+ hours calling tutoring centers one by one, and most centers don't have dyslexia-trained staff." The headline came out clean: "Find a tutor who actually understands dyslexia, 48 hours." She swapped the stock hero image for the WordPress screenshot. The calendar widget sat there with its red circle, an artifact from her own failure, now repurposed as evidence.
>
> She scanned the value props. Six bullet points: "calendar integration," "tutor profiles," "booking dashboard." Feature names her ICP had never used. She deleted all six. Rewrote four from the parent's perspective: "Book a dyslexia-trained tutor by Wednesday," "No more calling 8 centers to hear we don't have anyone." She read them aloud in a parent's voice. They sounded real.
>
> She sent the link to three parents in her network. All three typed their email into the waitlist without being asked. One replied: "The screenshot of the broken calendar, that was my screen yesterday. I almost cried." The page took 45 minutes. The hero image did the rest.
