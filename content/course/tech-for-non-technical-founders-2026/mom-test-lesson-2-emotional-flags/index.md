> **Micro-Lesson 2 of 3 · Module 2** · [From Idea to First Paying Customer](/course/tech-for-non-technical-founders-2026/)
>
> **Input:** your 5-8 question list from Lesson 1
>
> **Output:** the ability to spot frustration, workaround, and urgency language during an interview - and count flags to decide strong vs weak signal

---

**1. Hook (3 sentences):**

Your interviewee said "yes, I'd definitely use that" - the word "yes" told you nothing, because polite people say yes to be helpful. The three words that actually signal a real problem are "I hate this," "I pay $79/month for a workaround," and "this happened yesterday." In this lesson you'll learn to listen for emotional language instead of polite agreement.

**2. One-sentence outcome:**

After this lesson you will be able to: **identify frustration language, workaround language, and urgency language in an interview transcript, and count flags to separate strong signal from polite default.**

**3. Minimal concept block (180 words):**

While the 5 questions run, your job is to listen for three patterns. These flags do more work than the words "yes" and "no" the interviewee gives you.

**Flag 1: Frustration language**

| Example phrases | What it signals |
|---|---|
| "I hate this." "It drives me crazy." "Every single week." "I can't believe we still do it this way." | If the interviewee uses words with feeling, the problem is felt. Polite interviewees suppress feeling - the opposite of what validation needs. |

**Flag 2: Workaround language**

| Example phrases | What it signals |
|---|---|
| "I've been meaning to..." "We hacked together..." "I pay [tool] $X for this." "My VA does it manually." | Workarounds prove the problem is real because the interviewee already spent time or money on a solution that doesn't fully work. The workaround budget is the line item your product would replace. |

**Flag 3: Urgency language**

| Example phrases | What it signals |
|---|---|
| "Last week." "This morning." "I missed my kid's birthday because of this." | A problem that happened today is felt more sharply than a problem that happens "sometimes." Time-anchored urgency is the strongest signal in the set. |

**Scoring the flags:** A passing call has 3 or more flags spread across the five answers. A failing call has 0 or 1 - the interviewee is being polite to you. Two flags is ambiguous; treat as a 5/10 default until you calibrate against the Q4 score in Lesson 3.

**5. Do this now (4 steps, success check on step 4):**

1. Read this sample transcript excerpt aloud: *"Yeah, reporting is always a pain. We spend a lot of time on it. I've been meaning to look into a tool but haven't gotten around to it. Probably a 6 out of 10."*
2. Count the emotional flags. Frustration language? ("pain" - weak, no feeling words.) Workaround language? ("meaning to look into" - intention, not action. Not a real workaround.) Urgency language? (None - no dates, no "last week," no "this morning.")
3. Flag count: 0. This is a polite-default answer. Score it 4/10 - the interviewee is being helpful, not honest.
4. Now write down one frustration phrase, one workaround phrase, and one urgency phrase you'd expect to hear from someone who genuinely has your hypothesis's problem. **Success check:** Can you give a concrete quote for each? If you can't imagine the words, your hypothesis problem blank isn't specific enough - go back to [Lesson 1.1](/course/tech-for-non-technical-founders-2026/form-your-founding-hypothesis-90-minute-sprint/).

**6. Error recovery (Minimalist Instruction):**

**If this fails:** You can't hear any flags in your practice transcript - every answer sounds like polite filler. **Why:** you're listening for content ("what tool did they mention?") instead of emotion ("did they sound angry? relieved? rushed?"). The flags are in the word choice, not the facts. **Fix:** re-read the transcript and highlight every word that conveys feeling (hate, love, stuck, finally, always, never, yesterday, $). If you highlighted fewer than 3 words, the interviewee is being polite. If you highlighted 5+, the problem is real even if they said "maybe."

**7. Micro-reflection (1 question):**

- Think about the last time you complained about a real problem you have. What emotional words did you use? Write down the first phrase that comes to mind - that's what a real flag sounds like. Now compare it to your sample transcript from step 1.

**8. Bridge to next lesson:**

Next: you'll combine the 5 questions from Lesson 1 with these 3 flags to run and score a real 30-minute interview. Same script, same flags, scored within 5 minutes of hanging up.

> **Done when:** You can spot all 3 flag types in a transcript and count them to separate strong signal (3+ flags) from polite default (0-1 flags).

> **If blocked:** If the flags aren't clicking, pull up a G2 review for any SaaS product in your space. The 1-star and 5-star reviews are pure emotional language - frustration and urgency flags in the wild. Read 3 reviews and highlight every feeling word. The pattern will be obvious.

> **Case Study: Tomas & Mia**
>
> **Tomas** (B2B SaaS): Interview 3 - "My CFO bills $200/hr and spent 4 hours on reconciliation last week. That's $800." Flags: frustration ("$800"), workaround ("CFO bills $200/hr" - a hired person is the workaround), urgency ("last week"). 3 flags. Strong signal.
>
> **Mia** (B2C marketplace): Interview 5 - "I missed a $2,000 client deadline because I was on hold with a tutoring center." Flags: frustration ("missed a $2,000 client"), workaround ("on hold with a tutoring center" - the center is the workaround), urgency (implied: deadline-driven). 3 flags. Strong signal.
