> **Micro-Lesson 1 of 3 · Module 2** · [From Idea to First Paying Customer](/course/tech-for-non-technical-founders-2026/)
>
> **Input:** your Founding Hypothesis sentence (from Module 1)
>
> **Output:** a draft list of 5-8 past-tense questions with pass/fail signals for each

---

**1. Hook (3 sentences):**

You asked eleven people "would you pay for this?" and nine said yes - not one opened their wallet. The problem isn't your idea; it's your question. In this lesson you'll learn the 5 past-tense questions that force real answers out of polite people.

**2. One-sentence outcome:**

After this lesson you will be able to: **write a draft list of 5-8 Mom Test questions anchored in past behavior, with pass/fail signals for each.**

**3. Minimal concept block (280 words):**

Every bad interview question shares one flaw: it asks the interviewee to predict the future. "Would you use this?" "Would you pay $49/month?" Future-tense produces polite yeses - the interviewee can't refuse without being rude.

Past-tense questions produce real answers. "What did you do last Tuesday?" "What did the workaround cost?" Past-tense forces the answer to come from memory, not politeness.

**The 5 questions (read verbatim):**

> **Q1:** "Tell me about the **last time** [problem] happened. Walk me through what you did."
> - Pass: a specific story with a date, tool, and duration. Fail: "Yeah I usually struggle with reporting."
>
> **Q2:** "What did that **cost** you - in time, money, or sanity?"
> - Pass: a number with a unit. Fail: "It costs us time."
>
> **Q3:** "What have you **tried already** to fix this?"
> - Pass: a named tool, hired person, or duct-taped workaround. Fail: "Nothing yet."
>
> **Q4:** "On a scale of **1-10**, how big a problem is this compared to everything else on your plate?"
> - Pass: 7+ with a comparison to another priority. Fail: a bare "probably a 7" with no comparison.
>
> **Q5:** "**Who else** on your team feels this? How do they handle it?"
> - Pass: a specific colleague named plus their workaround. Fail: "I'm the only one who deals with this."

Read them as written. Improvise mid-call ("oh that reminds me of my product idea") and the interviewee switches from describing their life to pitching your product back to you.

**5. Do this now (4 steps, success check on step 4):**

1. Open a blank note. Copy the 5 questions above verbatim. The wording does the work - do not rephrase.
2. Replace every `[problem]` with the exact phrase from your Founding Hypothesis's problem blank (e.g. "insurance claim resubmission" - not "the billing issue").
3. Add 2-3 follow-ups specific to your ICP. Example for a chiropractor ICP: "Walk me through your last claim denial. Which code did the insurer reject?"
4. Read your list aloud. **Success check:** Is every question anchored in past behavior? If any question asks "would you," "do you think," or "how important is," rewrite it to ask about a specific past event. You know you're done when all 5-8 questions start with "Tell me about the last time" or "What did you do when."

**6. Error recovery (Minimalist Instruction):**

**If this fails:** Every question you wrote starts with "would you" or "do you find." **Why:** your brain defaults to testing the idea instead of testing the behavior. You're asking people to validate your product rather than describe their life. **Fix:** take your first question - e.g. "Would you use a tool for claim resubmission?" - and rewrite it as "Tell me about the last time you resubmitted a denied claim. Walk me through what you did." The past-tense version asks for a memory. The future-tense version asks for a guess.

**7. Micro-reflection (2 questions):**

- What is the first question on your list right now - and is it past-tense or future-tense?
- Save your draft question list in your `Founder OS` folder as `Mom Test draft - [today's date]`.

**8. Bridge to next lesson:**

Next: you'll learn the 3 emotional language flags that do more work than "yes" and "no." While the script runs, frustration language, workaround language, and urgency language tell you whether the problem is real - before you score a single answer.
