Linus Torvalds runs the most important codebase on Earth, and he just told the people writing it: if an AI helped you, fine, but a human name goes on the line. The Linux kernel will not accept a patch signed off by a model. Your dev shop probably will. That gap - between code an AI wrote and the human accountable for it - is the AI code ownership responsibility nobody warned you about.

You are a non-technical founder who already paid an agency to ship something. You can read a contract. You can read a commit log if somebody points at it. You cannot read C++. So when an investor's diligence partner emails on a Friday afternoon and asks "what percentage of your codebase was generated by AI, and who reviewed it?" you have to go ask the agency, and the agency has to go ask the developer who left in February, and by Monday morning you do not have an answer. The deal slips a week. Then it slips two.

## What Torvalds Did

In April 2026 the Linux kernel maintainers added a new tag, `Assisted-by:`, to the patch submission rules and codified a position that had been informal for a year. The [rules, written up in Hackaday on April 14, 2026](https://hackaday.com/2026/04/14/new-linux-kernel-rules-put-the-onus-on-humans-for-ai-tool-usage/), are short. A contributor may use an AI tool to draft a patch. The contributor must still personally read every line, sign it under the existing Developer Certificate of Origin (the DCO is the kernel's standing rule that whoever signs the patch is legally responsible for it), and stay liable if the code turns out to be plagiarized or licensed under terms the project cannot accept. Maintainers do not try to detect AI involvement. They enforce through review.

The kernel still accepts AI-drafted patches. The rule is that a human, not the model, signs the certificate of origin and absorbs the legal liability if the code turns out to be plagiarized or improperly licensed. A model cannot agree to that certificate because a model cannot be sued. So a person agrees, and that person stays on the hook for whatever the model wrote. "I let Claude write it" buys the contributor nothing. The signature still belongs to the human typing the commit.

The kernel is the operating system underneath roughly every server you have ever paid for. If the project that runs the world's data centers won't let an AI sign its own work, the project running your customers' subscriptions probably shouldn't either.

## Half The AI Code Out There Is Broken

Your app does not run the world's data centers. It still runs Stripe, holds customer email addresses, and collects money from people who assume somebody who knew what they were doing wrote the code on the other end of their browser. That somebody is partly Claude now, and Claude can't be deposed.

[Veracode's 2025 State of Software Security report](https://www.veracode.com/blog/research/state-of-software-security-2025-ai-generated-code/) found that 45% of AI-generated code samples - both the code the model wrote and the packages it suggested - contained at least one vulnerability from the OWASP Top 10, the standard list of things that get startups breached. The [Stanford 2025 AI Index Report](https://aiindex.stanford.edu/report/) tracks adoption of AI coding tools across professional developers: more than three quarters were using one at work by the end of 2025.

Half of AI-written code ships with a known security defect, and most of those developers are not running the kernel's review discipline. A lot of them work at the agency you hired.

## The Ownership Gap In Your Contract

Pull your dev shop contract out and look for the words "artificial intelligence" or "machine learning" or "generative." If they are not there, your contract is from before the question existed. That is most contracts. We have read a lot of agency master service agreements in the last six months on rescue engagements, and the gap is roughly universal. The IP-assignment clause says the deliverable belongs to you. The warranty clause says the work will be free of third-party claims. Neither clause says whether a human typed it or a model sampled it from somebody else's licensed source.

The agency promises you own the code, but does not promise to tell you who, or what, wrote it.

A founder we worked with in March 2026 - Series A B2B, Rails 7 stack, six months in - asked her agency a simple question after reading about the PocketOS incident. Which functions in our codebase were AI-assisted, who signed off on each one, and what dependencies did the AI pull in that nobody on her team has personally audited? Her agency's answer came back four days later and was, paraphrased: we cannot tell you, the developer who did most of it left in February, and his Cursor history is on his old laptop. She forwarded the email to us and asked what to do. The first thing we told her: that email is the audit finding. The second thing: her contract does not actually require the agency to keep that history at all, so the answer was technically compliant.

## What Investors Are Asking

A partner at a Bay Area seed fund told us in April that "AI code ratio" is now a standard line item on his technical diligence checklist for Series A and later. He skips past "did you use AI?" because everybody used AI. What he wants to hear on the call is "we know how much, and here is who signed off on each piece."

Founders who close the round answer on the call. The ones who don't, ship a follow-up email two weeks later and get a polite delay. We watched two companies lose term sheets in 2026 over follow-ups that were technically truthful and operationally embarrassing - one of them ours to advise, one of them not. The diligence partners we have spoken to are happy to back AI-heavy teams; they punish the founder who cannot describe what is in their own product.

Capital is following the gap. [Gitar raised $9 million in April 2026](https://techcrunch.com/2026/04/15/gitar-a-startup-that-uses-agents-to-secure-code-emerges-from-stealth-with-9-million/) to sell agents that audit AI-generated code for the issues humans are not catching, and Gitar is one of half a dozen companies in the same space. You do not have to buy Gitar to fix this in your own startup. The round confirms the shape of the problem the market thinks is real.

## Can You Name What's In Your Software?

There is one specific question the diligence partner asks that is worth memorizing. Can your team name every dependency in the codebase, including the ones the AI added without telling anyone?

This is the [Software Bill of Materials problem - the SBOM - and Security Boulevard wrote up the practitioner version in April 2026](https://securityboulevard.com/2026/04/vibe-coding-vs-sbom-one-builds-fast-the-other-tells-you-what-you-just-built/) with a sentence that should be on the wall of every founder's office: *"if you cannot name what is in your software, you do not control your software."*

Here is the shape of the problem. A model suggests a small piece of free third-party code to fix a bug. That piece pulls in six more from other strangers on the internet - the kind of transitive dependency chain Security Boulevard's piece describes, where an unmaintained library or a hijacked publishing account ends up in your build because the test suite went green and the ticket needed closing.

You did not consent to any of that, and you cannot list any of it either. You do not know who wrote the code. You do not know what code is in there. Both signatures are yours.

We covered the broader shape of this in [the vibe coding crisis post](/blog/vibe-coding-crisis-ai-code-debt/). "We shipped fast" is no longer a complete sentence in 2026 - "what exactly did you ship" travels with it.

## Five Clauses That Close The Gap

The cheapest fix is the contract you have not signed yet. Add these five clauses to the next master service agreement you put in front of an agency, or to the amendment you send the one you already have. None of them are exotic. A working agency will sign them without renegotiating rates. An agency that fights all five is telling you something useful.

*AI disclosure on every commit.* The agency agrees to use the kernel's `Assisted-by:` convention or its equivalent (a commit message tag, a separate column in their PR template) so each commit says whether AI tooling was involved and which one. Most days you will never read this. On the diligence call, it is your answer.

*Human reviewer of record.* Every AI-assisted commit names a person who read the diff before merge, and that person is liable for what the patch does. This is the clause that turns "the developer left in February" from a missing-person problem into a breach-of-contract problem.

Third clause: SBOM delivery on every release. The agency hands over a machine-readable list of every dependency in the codebase, including the ones that came along for the ride, with each release tag. Tools like Syft and CycloneDX produce this for free in under a day, so asking for it is not exotic. Refusing to deliver it is.

*Human approval on AI-introduced dependencies.* Any new package the AI suggests goes through a person before it lands in main. This will slow some merges by an hour or two; that is the cost, and it is worth it. The Veracode finding lives mostly here. A model picks the wrong package, a developer accepts the suggestion in a hurry, and the vulnerability rides in with it.

Last clause, the one that closes the back door: source-handover obligation on termination. You receive not only the code but the AI tooling history (Cursor sessions, saved prompts, a README listing which models were used in which periods). Your future investor will thank you. It also makes "we cannot tell you, that developer left in February" a breach instead of an apology.

Expect a 30-minute call about the first contract amendment, and possibly a small rate adjustment for the SBOM and handover clauses (those require operational changes on the agency's side). An agency that fights all five, though, is showing you exactly what was missing from the original deal.

## But Wait - You Trust Your Agency

Sure. So did Jer Crane at [PocketOS](/blog/ai-agent-deleted-production-database-pocketos/), a live car-rental SaaS whose AI agent dropped the production database and the backups in nine seconds. His agency was not malicious. They just did not gate the model the way the kernel gates a contributor. The diligence partner asks for a signature, not for trust. The kernel maintainers trust their contributors and still demand one. Without a paper trail, trust is just hope, and hope doesn't survive due diligence.

The last three codebases we inherited had no `Assisted-by:` history, no named human reviewer per commit, no SBOM, and no record of which dependencies the model had introduced. Each absence maps to one of the clauses above. None of them required exotic engineering - they required somebody asking the question before the contract was signed.

## How To Audit Yourself This Weekend

You do not need a forensic engineer to take the first pass. Here is what a non-technical founder can do.

Open your repository in your browser. Look at the commit history and read the last 50 messages out loud. If you cannot tell which of those changes were AI-assisted, your team is not tracking it. That is finding number one.

Then look at your agency's last invoice. Any line item reference AI tooling, prompts, or model usage? If not, ask the agency directly, by email, in writing: which of our commits this quarter used AI assistance, who reviewed each one, and what new dependencies were added as a result? Save the email - the reply is your audit document.

While you wait, open your hosting dashboard. Find out who can deploy to production and when somebody last tested restoring from a backup. PocketOS lost the live database and the offsite backup in nine seconds because the same access path opened both, and that path was an over-scoped agent token nobody had audited. Code ownership and credentials hygiene are the same audit; the post you are reading is half of it.

If the agency cannot answer the email in 48 hours, you have your answer.

## When Not To Bother

This whole conversation is overkill for a category of work that is genuinely fine to vibe-code: prototypes you will throw away in three weeks, internal scripts that touch one CSV and run once on a founder's laptop, marketing-page experiments, hackathon projects. The throwaway lane where Karpathy [originally framed vibe coding](/blog/vibe-coding-crisis-ai-code-debt/) is real and useful, and dragging it through a five-clause contract review will slow you down for no reward.

The line is whether the code touches production data or paying users. Below the line, ship fast and do not feel guilty. Above the line, the kernel's discipline applies and so do the five clauses. We have watched founders apply audit-grade rigor to a Notion-replacement side project and ship nothing for two quarters, which is its own failure mode. Pick your altitude before you pick the contract.

## What To Do Monday Morning

You do not have to fire anyone or switch agencies. What you have to do is ask one question in writing, get one answer in writing, and decide what to do based on the answer. If the agency can describe their AI policy in two paragraphs and produce an SBOM in 48 hours, your contract just needs the five clauses bolted on at renewal. If they cannot, [our guide to firing a dev shop](/blog/fire-dev-shop-guide/) covers what comes next, and [the questions to ask the next one](/blog/hiring-dev-shop-questions/) covers how to avoid landing here again.

Torvalds wrote down what every kernel maintainer already practiced: the human signs, the human is responsible, the model assists. Your codebase runs that rule whether the contract says so or not. You either find out on a weekend audit you ran yourself, or on a diligence call where the answer is somebody else's.
