## How to pick the right agency for your role

A few questions that filter the list quickly:

- **What kind of role?** A tech contract gig and a CFO search use different agencies. Korn Ferry runs retained executive searches; Insight Global places contractors in two weeks.
- **Permanent or contract?** Kelly Services and Aerotek live in contract and temp staffing. Robert Half does both. Korn Ferry is permanent only.
- **What's your timeline?** Contingency agencies (Randstad, Robert Half) get paid on placement, so they move fast on volume roles. Retained search (Korn Ferry) takes longer but goes deeper.
- **What's your budget?** Contingency fees are usually 15-25% of first-year salary. Retained search runs 25-33% paid in installments regardless of outcome.

## Profiles of the seven agencies

### Randstad

Dutch-headquartered global firm with a large US footprint. Places candidates in admin, finance, healthcare, manufacturing, and IT - mostly contingency, mostly mid-level. Useful when you have multiple roles open across functions and want one vendor.

### Kelly Services

US-based, founded 1946, and still one of the largest temp-staffing players. Strong in office/clerical, light industrial, and education. Good fit for short-term coverage and seasonal ramps.

### Insight Global

Atlanta-based staffing firm focused on IT contractors and contract-to-hire. Reports placing more than 50,000 job seekers a year across roughly 13,000 client companies. If you need a Java developer or a project manager on a contract starting next week, this is the lane.

### Robert Half

Specialist in finance, accounting, legal, and administrative roles. Operates several brands (Accountemps, OfficeTeam, Robert Half Technology) under one umbrella. Their consulting arm, Protiviti, picks up larger advisory work.

### Aerotek

An Allegis Group company. Places skilled trades, engineering, manufacturing, and logistics talent - the roles other firms struggle to fill. Heavy on contract and contract-to-hire.

### Korn Ferry

Retained executive search firm covering board, C-suite, and senior leadership roles. Combines search with leadership assessment and compensation consulting. Long process, high fee, deeper vetting than contingency search.

### Aquent

Creative and marketing talent: designers, copywriters, content strategists, UX, marketing ops. Mostly contract and freelance. Strong roster on the agency-side and in-house brand teams.

## When an agency is the wrong choice

Recruitment agencies make sense when you have a clear role definition and want someone else to handle sourcing. They're the wrong choice when:

- The role is genuinely niche and you're better served by a specialist boutique than a generalist with a wide net.
- You're hiring engineers and need someone who can technically vet them. Most agencies can't, which is why founders end up running their own technical screens anyway.
- You're a small startup and the contingency fee on a $120K hire ($18K-$30K) outweighs the time saved.

For early-stage technical hires, founders often get better results combining their network, a careful job spec, and a small focused recruiter who knows the specific stack - or by partnering with a development team that can ship while you hire.

## Trends to factor in for 2025

A handful of shifts that change how these agencies operate:

- **Remote-first roles** widen the candidate pool but also widen the competition. Agencies that still source mostly by metro area miss strong remote candidates.
- **AI screening** is now table stakes for high-volume sourcing. Useful for first-pass resume filtering, weak at assessing fit. Treat AI-only shortlists with skepticism.
- **Compensation transparency laws** in California, New York, Colorado, Washington, and others mean job posts need salary ranges. Agencies that try to keep compensation vague will lose candidates to ones that publish ranges up front.
- **Diversity sourcing** has matured from add-on to standard. Ask any agency you consider about their sourcing pipelines and placement data, not just their stated commitments.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are recruitment agencies?

Recruitment agencies connect job seekers with companies looking to hire. They handle sourcing, screening, and shortlisting in exchange for a fee from the hiring company.

### Why use a recruitment agency instead of hiring directly?

Speed and reach. Agencies have existing candidate pipelines, can run a search in parallel with your other work, and absorb the resume-screening load. The trade-off is fees and less control over how candidates are evaluated.

### What should I look for in a recruitment agency?

Industry track record, the actual recruiters you'll work with (not just the brand), how they screen, and whether their placements stick. Ask for references from clients who hired similar roles in the past 12 months.

### How do recruitment agencies find candidates?

A mix of internal databases, LinkedIn outreach, referrals, niche job boards, and proactive headhunting for senior roles. Bigger firms lean on AI sourcing tools; smaller boutiques rely more on relationships.

### Are recruitment agencies only for big companies?

No. Most US agencies work with startups and small businesses. Contingency models in particular work well for one-off hires - you only pay if they place someone.

### What types of jobs do recruitment agencies fill?

Most office, technical, healthcare, finance, industrial, and executive roles. The right agency depends on the role: tech contract roles, executive search, and creative work all have specialists who do them better than generalist firms.
